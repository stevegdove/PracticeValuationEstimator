Does that overhead % include what you pay yourself as the owner-doctor?
?
Dentists report overhead two ways, and it changes your EBITDA a lot:
Excludes your pay (common for solo docs): overhead is every cost except your own clinical income. The typical GP runs ~62% this way (ADA HPI mean 61.3%). We'll subtract a market-rate replacement dentist for you.
Includes your pay (common when you take a formal W-2 salary): your compensation is already inside the overhead figure, so what's left is closer to true profit. We won't subtract it again.
Why it matters: say you're solo at "70% overhead / 30% profit" but never counted your own pay. A buyer pays a dentist ~30% of production to replace you — so your real EBITDA is near zero, i.e. ~100% overhead. Getting this right is the difference between a real DSO offer and none.

Hygiene not included. If you're solo with no associates, this is usually 100%. This tells us how much of the practice leans on you — the single best-documented value driver — and, when your pay is excluded above, how big a replacement-dentist cost to subtract.

5Large equipment

Check what the practice owns. Modern equipment means the buyer isn't facing a re-equip bill, so it moves you up within the range — it doesn't stack on top as a separate line item, because buyers bake it into the price. The list adjusts to your practice type.

6Facility & curb appeal

A turnkey, attractive office on a visible road generates its own new patients and spares the buyer a remodel — both push you up within the range. Owning the building doesn't change the practice's value, but it's a separate asset you could sell or lease back; we'll note it rather than price it here.

7One assumption you can change

To figure what the practice earns without you, buyers subtract what they'd pay a dentist to do your clinical work — the common industry rule of thumb is 28–32% of production for a GP. Adjust it if you know your market rate.
